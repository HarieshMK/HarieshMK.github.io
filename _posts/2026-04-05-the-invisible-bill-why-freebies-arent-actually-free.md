The bicycle, the mixer, the television, the fridge, or that ₹2,000 hitting your bank account—it’s all free, right? 

That’s what they want you to think. Imagine this: If a random stranger out of nowhere offers you a free meal, would you accept it without a second thought? There’s always a catch. In this blog, we’re going to see how you are actually paying the price for those "freebies."

## The Hard Truth
Let’s cut the crap first. The government isn't spending a paisa from their own pockets. It’s your money. How? 

Have you seen any politicians selling their land or personal assets to provide you with that bicycle? Or spending their own salary to fund that ₹2,000 you receive every month? Well, I don’t think so. There are only two sources for this money:
1. **The taxes you pay.**
2. **The debt they take.**

The freebie you get today is just you signing a 5-year contract with your own future.

“But bro, I’m getting taxed anyway! I might as well get my share of the freebies! Why should I let only others enjoy the freebies for which I have also paid for?” 

I get it, that’s a natural feeling. But the more freebies that get distributed, the more money they have to print and the more debt they have to take. If you can afford the lifestyle without these freebies, respectfully neglect them.

## The Inflation Trap
You might think, "If the government can print money, isn't that good for the economy?" 

Technically, no. Let’s look at an example. Imagine a village with only **one bag of rice** and 10 villagers. Each has ₹100, so the price of the rice is capped by that reality. Now, the government gives every villager a ₹1,000 freebie. Everyone now has ₹1,100, but there is still only **one** bag of rice. Naturally, the auction for that rice will jump to ₹1,100.



The price goes up because more money is chasing the same amount of goods. This is inflation. The freebie you got yesterday just increased the price of the rice you buy tomorrow. Now, this is not the exact textbook definition of inflation, but it is the rough reality of how it hits your pocket. It’s not an exaggeration. 

## Beyond Inflation: The Cost of Stagnation
Inflation isn't the only issue. By chasing these freebies, we are missing out on so much more. That debt could have built a better school, a safer road to avoid accidents, or a hospital to keep you safe. But we chose the short term. We want everything today, so we forget about tomorrow.

And who pays that debt? You and your children. Because inflation doesn't happen overnight—it slowly poisons the economy. Just think about what price your father purchased a bag of rice or a piece of land for years ago versus what it costs today. You’ll have your answer.

I hope you’re seeing the news about government teachers, bus drivers, and hospital staff being underpaid? No hikes are being given and pension amounts are being cut. These are the indications that your money is being spent somewhere uselessly instead of where it matters. 

Imagine yourself in a situation where you are underpaid. Will you give your 100% to that work? No, right? That’s why the government facilities in Tamil Nadu (and elsewhere) feel broken. We sacrificed their growth for a mixer.

## Good Debt vs. Bad Debt
Now, not all debts are bad. If the government borrows to build a hospital, a good school, or to develop a tourist spot, that generates income. Those things are permanent. They pay you and the government eventually. 



But a free mixer? It’s operational for a year, and the ₹2,000 cash is spent in a day. Ironically, the money they give you usually finds its way back to them sooner than you think—through the taxes you pay on the very things you buy with that "free" money.

You all complain that you’re paying too much tax but not getting anything in return. Well, how did you feel when you got that free laptop? That is the trade-off. These freebies excite us for one day, but good infrastructure excites generations.

## What Should We Do?
"Bro, what should I do? I don’t want only others to enjoy the freebies I paid for."

Simple. **Select your leaders correctly.** In this country, the only real right we have is our vote. If they say "free bus tickets for ladies," and you can afford it, buy a ticket for your lady. The freebies are optional, not mandatory.

Yes, one vote can’t change everything. But we can educate. We can tell our families that it’s not just two parties competing; it doesn't have to be this way. Share this article, share the truth. Not just this article—if you see any YouTube videos or any other articles or Instagram posts about this, share it. Sometimes we have to wide-open people's eyes for us all to survive.

Remember, 'Rome was not built in a day.' The one small step we take today is the biggest leap for our next generation. Let’s build a good planet for them.
